# Brand Patterns

Extracted from analysing 88 brands across 11 dimensions. These patterns are referenced by the brandkit skills as methodology grounding.

---

## Archetypes

### Frequency (88 brands)

| Primary Archetype | Count | Typical Categories |
|---|---|---|
| The Creator | 29 | technology, luxury, entertainment, consumer-hardware |
| The Explorer | 10 | travel-transport, sport-outdoor, automotive |
| The Hero | 8 | sport-outdoor, food-beverage |
| The Magician | 8 | fintech, entertainment, health-wellness |
| The Everyman | 7 | e-commerce, food-beverage, fashion-retail |
| The Sage | 6 | education, technology |
| The Caregiver | 5 | health-wellness, e-commerce |
| The Outlaw | 4 | food-beverage, fintech |
| The Ruler | 4 | luxury, automotive |
| The Jester | 3 | education, social-community |
| The Innocent | 2 | beauty, food-beverage |
| The Lover | 2 | beauty, food-beverage |

### Common Pairings

Creator + Sage — technology brands that build and educate (Stripe, Notion, Figma)
Creator + Magician — luxury brands that transform through craft (Chanel, Hermes)
Hero + Magician — performance brands with aspirational reach (Nike)
Explorer + Creator — brands that discover and build (Airbnb, Rivian)
Outlaw + Jester — challenger brands with humour (Oatly, Duolingo)
Ruler + Creator — heritage luxury with craft authority (Rolex, Louis Vuitton)

### Pairings That Never Appear

- Ruler + Jester (authority and irreverence conflict)
- Caregiver + Outlaw (nurturing and rebellion conflict)
- Innocent + Ruler (naivety and control conflict)

### Category Patterns

- **Luxury** is Creator or Ruler, never Jester or Everyman
- **Fintech** splits: Magician (transformation) or Outlaw (disruption)
- **Sport-outdoor** is Hero or Explorer, never Sage or Caregiver
- **Entertainment** is always Creator, with Magician or Sage secondary
- **Education** splits: Sage (knowledge) or Jester (engagement)

## Positioning Scales

### What Scores Mean

**Utility ↔ Theater (1-10)**
- 1-3: Product IS the message. Function-first. (Amazon 2, CVS 2, Domino's 2)
- 4-6: Experience amplifies the product. Design as advantage. (Nike 6, Apple 6)
- 7-10: Brand IS the product. You buy the story. (Chanel 9, Disney 9, HBO 9)

**Access ↔ Exclusivity (1-10)**
- 1-3: Available to everyone. Price leadership. (Amazon 1, McDonald's 1, H&M 2)
- 4-6: Aspirational-accessible. Premium but not gated. (Nike 5, Apple 6)
- 7-10: Scarcity is the strategy. The waitlist IS the product. (Hermes 10, Chanel 10)

**Heritage ↔ Innovation (1-10)**
- 1-3: We don't change. That's the point. (Coca-Cola 2, Hermes 2, Rolex 2)
- 4-6: Tradition as foundation, not cage. (Patagonia 4, Aesop 4)
- 7-10: Inventing the future. History irrelevant. (Tesla 10, OpenAI 10, all fintech 7-9)

### Category Averages

| Category | Utility/Theater | Access/Exclusivity | Heritage/Innovation |
|---|---|---|---|
| luxury | 8.0 | 9.0 | 4.0 |
| fintech | 4.5 | 2.6 | 8.8 |
| technology | 5.0 | 4.0 | 8.6 |
| entertainment | 7.3 | 3.9 | 7.3 |
| sport-outdoor | 5.0 | 4.2 | 6.4 |
| automotive | 6.8 | 6.8 | 7.4 |
| food-beverage | 4.4 | 3.4 | 6.4 |
| health-wellness | 3.7 | 3.4 | 7.7 |

### Tight Categories (everyone agrees)

- **Social-community**: all score U/T=6, A/E 2-4, H/I 7-8. Most conformist.
- **Fashion-retail**: A/E tightly 2-3. All deeply democratic.
- **Fintech**: A/E 2-4, H/I 7-9. Differentiation happens on U/T only.

### Spread Categories (diverse strategies)

- **Automotive**: Ford (4/3/4) to Jaguar (9/9/8). Entire spectrum.
- **Food-beverage**: Coca-Cola (3/2/2) to Oatly (7/4/9). Heritage to disruption.
- **Beauty**: Aesop (6/7/4) to Glossier (4/4/7). Heritage-exclusive to innovation-accessible.

### Outlier Brands (breaks category pattern)

- **Patagonia** in sport-outdoor: lowest theater (3), highest exclusivity (6). Anti-Nike.
- **MasterClass** in education: luxury brand trapped in education (8/7/7).
- **Tesla** in automotive: only brand scoring 10 on any scale (innovation).
- **Square** in fintech: massively high theater (8) where all other fintech is 3-6.
- **Seed Health** in health: luxury positioning inside health (6/7/9).

### White Space (no brand occupies)

- High theater + high access + high innovation (8+/1-2/8+)
- Low theater + high exclusivity + high innovation (1-3/8+/8+)
- The exact center (5/5/5) — every brand picks a direction

## Visual Clusters

### Distribution (88 brands)

| Cluster | Count | Positioning Signature |
|---|---|---|
| geometric-clean | 21 | moderate theater, moderate access, innovation-forward |
| bold-challenger | 13 | mid theater, accessible, innovation-forward |
| dark-premium | 11 | mid theater, moderate access, very high innovation |
| warm-humanist | 11 | lower theater, accessible, moderate innovation |
| editorial-luxury | 9 | high theater, high exclusivity, heritage-leaning |
| heritage-craft | 4 | low theater, moderate exclusivity, strong heritage |
| cinematic-narrative | 3 | high theater, moderate exclusivity, balanced |
| expressive-maximalist | 3 | highest theater, high exclusivity, balanced |
| soft-minimal | 4 | low theater, accessible, innovation-forward |
| tech-system | 3 | low-mid theater, maximum access, innovation-forward |

### Cluster × Positioning Rules

- If U/T > 7 AND A/E > 7 → editorial-luxury or expressive-maximalist
- If H/I > 8 AND A/E < 4 → dark-premium, bold-challenger, or geometric-clean
- If H/I < 4 AND A/E > 7 → editorial-luxury or heritage-craft
- Heritage-craft is the only cluster pulling LOW on both theater and innovation

### Category Norms

- **Fintech**: split geometric-clean / dark-premium / bold-challenger (Monzo is only warm-humanist)
- **Technology**: geometric-clean dominant (Figma breaks with expressive-maximalist)
- **Luxury**: editorial-luxury dominant (Gucci breaks with expressive-maximalist)
- **Food-beverage**: heritage brands go warm-humanist, challenger brands go bold-challenger

### Common Pairings

- cinematic-narrative + geometric-clean (Nike, HBO)
- editorial-luxury + geometric-clean (luxury brands for digital)
- dark-premium + geometric-clean (Stripe, Spotify)
- bold-challenger + warm-humanist (Oatly, Duolingo)

## Voice Patterns

### Top Anti-Trait (universal)

"Corporate" is rejected by 14 of 35 brands analysed. The most universal anti-pattern.

### Trait Clusters That Appear Together

| Cluster | Traits | Example Brands |
|---|---|---|
| Confident Restraint | confident, sophisticated, refined, understated | Hermes, Chanel, Rolex |
| Bold Honesty | bold, honest, direct, provocative | Oatly, Wise, Tesla, Patagonia |
| Warm Community | inclusive, community-focused, approachable, genuine | Airbnb, Glossier, Strava, Discord |
| Quiet Precision | precise, understated, clear, considered | Stripe, Uniqlo, Aesop, Notion |
| Aspirational Empowerment | inspiring, empowering, aspirational, motivating | Nike, MasterClass, Fender |

### Traits That Never Co-Exist

- playful + sophisticated
- provocative + refined
- minimal + community-focused
- rebellious + knowledgeable (rare exception: Wise)
- conversational + commanding

### Best Trait + Boundary Pairs

| Brand | Trait | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Apple | authoritative | never arrogant |
| Stripe | confident | never flashy |
| Chanel | sophisticated | never pretentious |
| Patagonia | urgent | never preachy |
| Oatly | provocative | never corporate |
| Duolingo | friendly nagging | never shame-based |
| Rolex | confident | never boastful |
| Wise | direct | never euphemistic |

### Best always_sound Guardrails (character-type, not trait lists)

- Patagonia: "Like someone who has been outside and wants to protect what is out there"
- Stripe: "Like the smartest engineer who explains things simply"
- Tesla: "Like the future arrived early and has no patience for the past"
- Spotify: "Like a music-obsessed friend with impeccable taste"
- Wise: "Like someone who has studied banking thoroughly, found it overcharges..."

The character-type guardrails are far more useful than trait lists. They give a writer an immediate mental model — a person you could imagine meeting.

## feels_like Quality Bar

### Top 5

1. **Aesop**: "A bookshop that happens to sell skincare. The kind of place where the staff have read the books on the shelf and would rather talk about Tanizaki than moisturiser."
2. **Patagonia**: "A conversation at a campfire with someone who has walked the planet and come back angry about what they've seen."
3. **Airbnb**: "The moment you realize the stranger who picked you up at the airport is now showing you the hidden cafe their grandmother took them to as a child."
4. **Oatly**: "The punk rock band that played your high school cafeteria and suddenly made you realize the system you'd accepted was negotiable."
5. **Duolingo**: "A coach that texts you at exactly the wrong time and somehow knows it will make you smile instead of flinch."

### What Makes Them Work

1. **Scene before thesis** — open with a concrete scene, then pivot to meaning
2. **Specific sensory detail** — at least one hyper-specific detail (a name, a number, a texture)
3. **The reader is in the scene** — use "you" inside the experience
4. **Paradox in the description** — the feeling contains a tension the brand resolves
5. **Metaphor from outside the category** — skincare as bookshop, food brand as punk band
6. **Transformation narrative** — the reader enters one state and exits another

### What Makes Them Fail

- Abstract thesis with no scene ("invisible life force become visible")
- Dead similes ("like electricity")
- Marketing copy instead of felt experience ("distraction-free clarity made tangible")
- Generic language that could apply to any brand in the category

## Creative Tension Patterns

### Structural Types

**1. Commerce ↔ Philosophy**
Nike: manufactures shoes but worships performance. Commerce vs transcendence.
Oatly: values-driven but owned by Blackstone. Mission vs capital.
Aesop: refusal of scale, now owned by world's largest beauty conglomerate.

**2. Heritage ↔ Innovation**
Chanel: worships innovation but refuses to look like it's trying.
Disney: heritage icon since 1923 vs constant storytelling innovation.
Coca-Cola: 138-year heritage vs constant cultural reinvention.

**3. Accessibility ↔ Exclusivity**
Rolex: must feel unattainable yet accessible enough that someone actually wears it daily.
Prada: ideas are free; execution costs everything.
Coursera: democratised access vs premium university-quality positioning.

**4. Scale ↔ Intimacy**
Booking.com: infinite choice (3.4M properties) vs effortless simplicity.
Sweetgreen: scaled enterprise vs integrity of direct farmer relationships.
Spotify: democratise discovery yet requires massive data aggregation.

**5. Simplicity ↔ Sophistication**
Stripe: unlimited sophistication for technical users, beginner-friendly onboarding.
Notion: freedom without structure = chaos, too much structure destroys value.
Apple: radical simplicity housing extraordinary complexity.

### Best Examples (vivid, brand-specific)

- Nike: "manufactures shoes; athletes manufacture meaning"
- Aesop: "refusal of scale, now owned by the company that paid $2.525B for it"
- Prada: "ideas are free; execution costs everything"
- Oatly: "punk band playing your cafeteria — the system is negotiable"
- Nothing: "retro-futurism (80s IBM mainframe) speaking to contemporary design"
- Chanel: "revolutionary choices that appear inevitable in retrospect"

## Anti-Pattern Library

### Universal (10+ brands)

1. Corporate speak in place of human language
2. Celebrity endorsement without authentic connection
3. Competing on price / commodification
4. Trend-chasing / fashion sensitivity
5. Fear / guilt / shame-based messaging

### By Type

**Visual**: thermal inversion (warm brand goes cold), breaking colour discipline, staged photography where documentary is required, abandoning typography anchor

**Strategic**: the comparison trap (arguing vs competitor elevates them), growth-vs-soul dilemma (expansion that dilutes), authenticity inversion (explaining magic kills it), premiumisation trap (adding tiers that violate egalitarian positioning)

**Tonal**: earnestness without specificity, jargon replacing human language, guilt/shame boundary violation, over-performed authority

**Cultural**: absorbing trends you predate, democratising when exclusion is the strategy, corporate content where community voice is required
